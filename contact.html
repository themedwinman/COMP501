How BCIs Can Help

The World Health Organization reports that approximately 16% of the global population lives with some form of disability, ranging from motor impairments to communication challenges and mental health conditions. For many of these individuals, daily life is limited by their physical or neurological conditions. Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCIs) are emerging as a technology with the potential to restore independence, communication, and improve quality of life. BCIs can help enable movement in paralyzed individuals, support those recovering from head injury, and support mental health monitoring. BCIs are just having a computer in the user’s brain, but could allow access to a future alongside society.

Medical Applications

Brain-Computer Interfaces have the potential to revolutionize medicine and rehabilitation, especially for individuals living with serious physical or neurological conditions. By reading brain activity and translating it into data that can be read by doctors to understand their patients. BCIs create new ways for patients to interact with their surroundings and society. These systems can perform tasks based on neural signals the BCIs read for further exploration of the human brain and mental illness, or hidden illnesses not yet discovered. The information gathered not only supports immediate medical needs but also contributes to long-term research, helping us explore the brain more deeply and develop more effective treatments for born or acquired disabilities.

Paralysis and Motor Impairment

BCIs can be used to help users with paralysis or severe motor impairments interact with technologies such as typing on a keyboard. The World Health Organisation states that in 2021, over 15 million people had spinal cord injuries such as paralysis, ALS, or severe motor disorders, but with the use of BCIs, they can regain interaction with the world. These systems enable users to operate wheelchairs, communicate through speech software, or control prosthetics using thought alone.

Communication for Locked-In Patients

For those with conditions like locked-in syndrome or advanced ALS, BCIs are one of the only ways to communicate. A two-year study conducted at the Wyss Centre for Bio and Neuron Engineering in Geneva, Switzerland, demonstrated that a man with advanced amyotrophic lateral sclerosis, who was completely locked-in and nonverbal, was able to communicate using an implanted BCI system. This achievement not only answered long-standing questions about the potential for communication in individuals with complete paralysis but also opened the door to new possibilities for those living with severe neurological conditions.

Through focused thought, users can select letters or words on a digital keyboard, providing a vital connection to the world around them.

Epilepsy Monitoring and Seizure Prevention

With BCI technologies being able to track and record the user's brain patterns and normal functions, it will become a reliable method of understanding seizures and can help with pre-emptive warning before they strike.

The current use of EEG technologies is being used on drug-resistant epilepsy patients to record what happens within their brain while on the medication, but what are the everyday updates, and the scope of the data has increased significantly.

Mental Health and Emotional Monitoring

As we stated, BCIs can record and track data from the user’s brain. Some BCIs are being developed to assist in therapy by monitoring brain activity linked to anxiety, focus, or mood. These systems could eventually help regulate emotional responses through feedback or targeted stimulation. This can help diagnose and support people with mental illnesses and explore in depth the greater understanding of these illnesses.

Stroke Recovery and Rehabilitation

In New Zealand, Stroke Aotearoa recorded that 1 in 4 Kiwis over the age of 25 will suffer a stroke, with the ones that survive likely having motor dysfunctions such as speech impairments, etc. The World Stroke Organization's fact sheet from 2022 released a graph of the daily mortality and disability of all stroke types.

BCIs are actively tested in stroke therapy to help patients regain lost motor functions. When a person imagines moving or talking, a BCI can detect that signal a help the brain rebuild motor pathways to improve speech or mobility.

Risks of BCIs

Brain Computer interfaces may seem like the grand entrance to a whole new era of sci-fi technology, but in some cases, it’s far from it. There are many different risk factors involved with using BCIs, alongside adverse side effects that no one seems to be mentioning. The most obvious risks are surgical complications mid or post operation, especially when it comes to invasive BCIs such as the implant being developed at Neuralink. Another factor that needs to be considered is the body’s response to the implant. According to (Medical Xpress, 2023), “If a hard metallic or plastic device is implanted into the soft tissue of the brain, these small, normal movements can lead to friction and inflammation of the tissues around the implant, killing off vital brain cells and causing scarring.” This source offers insight into the damage the implant might be causing patients. It is essential to note that animal trials were conducted before human trials to confirm the safety of the initial surgery. However, long-term (10+ years) complications or side effects have not been studied or observed, as BCIs have not been in use for that long.

BCIs don’t just carry physical/surgical risk either. Alongside the obvious physical health concerns, there could be unforeseen psychological and emotional factors playing roles in a patient’s well-being. Some of the most notable being the loss of mental autonomy and an emerging over-reliance on technology. Users of an implanted BCI may feel like their thoughts are no longer private or even within their control. This is more prominent in “closed-loop” BCIs, which can both read and stimulate neuron activity. We live in an era where smartphone use is at an all-time high, and according to Elhai et al. (2017), “Excessive smartphone use is associated with increased perceived stress, poor sleep, and lower psychological well-being.” When we consider that BCIs can mimic the effects of smartphones while directly injecting their controls into the brain, losing the barrier of a physical input, we could imagine that there might be adverse psychological effects. These could include, but are not limited to, cognitive overload and mental fatigue, overreliance on the BCI, and other unintended consequences such as mood swings and anxiety. Due to the intense concentration required to effectively use a BCI, to someone who has not used one before, it can take a lot of effort and often lead to periods of significant mental fatigue and distress.

While there are many unknown risk factors that come along with Brain-Computer Interfaces, there are an overwhelming number of positives within medicine, consumer entertainment, and scientific research.
